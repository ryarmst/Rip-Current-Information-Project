FAQ

Common questions about RiP and Great Lakes rip currents.

These answers are intended to help families, educators, partners, and media understand RiP's work and core safety messages.

Are you a charity?

RiP is a Canadian non-profit public safety initiative. Formal charitable giving options may expand over time; for now, donations are accepted through the linked GoFundMe, with CanadaHelps noted as a future option.

Are your programs really free?

Yes. RiP is committed to keeping water safety education free and accessible, especially for schools and communities. Donations and partnerships help remove barriers.

How long are the presentations?

The "Understanding Rip Currents" presentation is approximately 60 minutes. Sessions can be adapted for classrooms, assemblies, municipal meetings, conferences, and beach-based learning.

Are the presentations suitable for young children?

Yes. Presentations are adjusted for age and audience. Younger learners focus on recognizing unsafe conditions, knowing when not to enter the water, and learning simple self-rescue principles without fear-based messaging.

Do rip currents really happen on the Great Lakes?

Yes. Many people associate rip currents with oceans, but they occur on the Great Lakes and can form quickly when wind, waves, sandbars, and shoreline structures concentrate water moving away from shore.

Do rip currents pull people under water?

No. Rip currents are strongest at the surface and pull people away from shore, not under the water. The danger comes from panic, exhaustion, and not knowing how to respond.

What should I do if I am caught in a rip current?

Stay calm, float to conserve energy, and do not fight directly against the current. If possible, swim parallel to shore to leave the current, then angle back with the waves. Wave and call for help if needed.

Is calm-looking water always safe?

No. Calm-looking water can sometimes mark deeper channels or water moving offshore, especially after storms or near structures. Check wind, waves, posted warnings, and local conditions before entering.
