Rip Currents 101

Know what a rip current is before the water makes the lesson urgent.

Rip currents are powerful, narrow streams of water that flow away from shore. They can quickly carry even strong swimmers into deeper water.

What Is a Rip Current?

A rip current forms when waves push water toward the beach faster than it can return naturally, forcing water to funnel back out through a deeper or weaker point in the nearshore zone.

  • Between sandbars or through deeper channels.
  • Near structures like breakwalls, rock formations, jetties, groins, and piers.
  • Where wave patterns and lake-bottom features concentrate moving water.

Rip currents are not undertows.

Rips do not pull people under the water. They are strongest at the surface and pull people away from shore, leading to exhaustion, panic, and drowning when people do not know how to respond.

How to Spot One

Rip currents are often difficult to see, especially on the Great Lakes, but there are common warning signs to watch for.

  • A noticeable break or gap in incoming waves.
  • A channel of darker or murkier water extending away from shore.
  • Debris or sediment moving steadily offshore.
  • A section of water that looks choppy, rippled, unusually calm, or otherwise different.

When in doubt, stay out of the water or swim near lifeguards when available.

Conditions can change quickly, and rip currents may be present even when the beach looks calm.

What to Do If You Are Caught

  • Stay calm and try not to fight the current.
  • Float to conserve energy.
  • If possible, swim parallel to the shoreline to escape a narrow or slower channel rip.
  • Once free, angle back toward shore with the waves.
  • Use a sandbar, when safe and available, as a place to catch your breath.

If you see someone in trouble

Do not enter the water. Call for help, alert emergency services, and throw something that floats if possible.

Float first. Fighting the current wastes the energy you need to get home.

Great Lakes Context

Freshwater does not mean current-free.

Many people do not associate large freshwater lakes with rip currents, which can create a false sense of security. Great Lakes waves are driven by wind, crash in quicker succession than ocean waves, and can change rapidly.

Piers, breakwalls, and jetties can intensify rip currents, creating fast-moving water close to structures and shore. Understanding these differences is critical for anyone recreating on or near the Great Lakes.

Types of Rip Currents

Rip currents form in different ways.

Bathymetrically Controlled

Controlled by the shape and contours of the lake bottom, including nearshore or offshore features.

Channelized rip currents

These occupy deeper channels that interrupt shallow, mostly shore-parallel sandbars. They can persist in a relatively fixed location for days, weeks, or months.

Focused rip currents

These occur at relatively fixed locations and are controlled by alongshore variations in breaking waves created by offshore bathymetric features.

Structurally Controlled

These occur adjacent to human-made structures such as breakwalls, jetties, and piers, and can occur even during small breaking wave conditions.

Shadow rip currents

These flow on the sheltered side of a rigid structure or boundary, where wave setup differences force water back toward the obstacle and then offshore.

Deflection rip currents

These occur when waves breaking at an angle create longshore currents that are physically deflected lakeward by a structure.

Hydrodynamically Controlled

These are controlled by wave and current interactions and may occur on featureless straight beaches.

Flash rip currents

Also called transient rips, these can form when short-crested waves drive small swirling flows that merge, often lasting for several minutes.

Shear-instability rip currents

These form when eddies are shed off longshore currents as a result of waves breaking at an angle to the coast.
